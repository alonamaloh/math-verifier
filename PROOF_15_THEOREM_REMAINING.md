# A detailed proof of the final seven rank-four covers

## 0. Claim, scope, and dependency boundary

This document proves the following statement.

### Main theorem

Assume the two classical ternary results stated precisely below:

1. the genus representation results for the determinant-seven genera used by
   Jones, Pall, and Kaplansky; and
2. Legendre's three-square theorem.

Then each of the seven quadratic forms
\[
Q_{0,7},\ Q_{1,6},\ Q_{1,7},\ Q_{2,8},\ Q_{2,9},\ Q_{3,10},\ Q_{3,11}
\]
defined in Section 4 is universal.

These are exactly the seven selected rank-four forms for which the current
Fifteen-Theorem development does not yet have a conditional universality
proof.  Thus this argument closes the **rank-four coverage problem**.  It
does not, by itself, formalize the classical ternary theorems on which the
other rank-four covers also depend.  Section 8 gives the complete dependency
ledger so that this distinction cannot be missed.

The proof has three kinds of ingredient:

| kind | treatment here |
| --- | --- |
| elementary identities and lattice arguments | proved line by line |
| finite congruence and representation checks | algorithm and exhaustive bounds stated; executable checker supplied |
| classical ternary representation theorems | exact statements and exact sources supplied; no claim that they are already formalized |

Every finite calculation in this document is reproduced by
[`scripts/check_det7_human_proof.js`](scripts/check_det7_human_proof.js).
It uses exact integer arithmetic and has no package dependencies.

The principal published sources are:

- Manjul Bhargava, [*On the Conway--Schneeberger Fifteen
  Theorem*](https://www.fen.bilkent.edu.tr/~franz/mat/15.pdf), especially
  Section 4 and Tables 1--2;
- Irving Kaplansky, [*The first nontrivial genus of positive definite ternary

- Gordon Pall, [*An almost universal
  form*](https://doi.org/10.1090/S0002-9904-1940-07200-3), p. 291.

## 1. Representation, least exceptions, and orthogonal residuals

For a symmetric integral matrix \(A\), write
\[
q_A(v)=v^{\mathsf T}Av.
\]
The form \(A\) **represents** an integer \(n\) if \(q_A(v)=n\) for some
integral vector \(v\).  It is **universal** if it represents every positive
integer.

### Lemma 1.1: a least positive exception is squarefree

Let \(Q\) be an integral quadratic form.  If \(n\) is the least positive
integer not represented by \(Q\), then \(n\) is squarefree.

**Proof.**
Suppose that \(p^2\mid n\) for a prime \(p\), and write \(n=p^2s\).  Then
\(s\) is a positive integer smaller than \(n\), so minimality gives an
integral vector \(v\) with \(Q(v)=s\).  Since a quadratic form is homogeneous
of degree two,
\[
Q(pv)=p^2Q(v)=p^2s=n.
\]
This contradicts the definition of \(n\). \(\square\)

Two consequences used later are
\[
n\not\equiv0\pmod4
\quad\text{and}\quad
n\not\equiv0\pmod {49}.
\]

### Lemma 1.2: the orthogonal residual principle

Let \(L\) be an integral quadratic lattice containing an orthogonal
sublattice
\[
T\perp\langle m\rangle,
\]
where the last summand is generated by an integral vector \(v\) of norm
\(m>0\).  If \(T\) represents \(n-mt^2\) for some integer \(t\), then \(L\)
represents \(n\).

**Proof.**
Choose \(u\in T\) with \(Q(u)=n-mt^2\).  Orthogonality gives
\[
Q(u+tv)=Q(u)+t^2Q(v)=n-mt^2+mt^2=n.
\]
Both \(u\) and \(tv\) lie in \(L\), so this is an integral representation by
\(L\). \(\square\)

Notice what this lemma does **not** require.  We do not need to know every
integer represented by \(T\).  We need only a proven subset of its
represented integers and enough freedom in \(t\) to move every large target
into that subset.

## 2. The determinant-seven ternary theorem

Put
\[
g(x,y,z)=x^2+2y^2+2yz+4z^2.
\]
Its Gram matrix and determinant are
\[
G=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&0\\
0&2&1\\
0&1&4
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\det G=7.
\]

The form \(g\) is not regular: local representability alone does not
characterize its represented integers.  We therefore use only a specific
published subset of the integers it represents.

### 2.1 The exact classical genus input

Call a positive integer \(N\) **7-eligible** if it is not of the form
\[
N=7^{\,2a+1}u,
\qquad
a\ge0,\quad 7\nmid u,\quad
u\equiv3,5,\text{ or }6\pmod7.
\]
The residues \(3,5,6\) are exactly the nonzero quadratic nonresidues modulo
7.  Equivalently, the forbidden shape has odd 7-adic valuation and a
nonresidue unit part.

We use the following classical result.

### Classical genus result D7

Define
\[
\begin{aligned}
f(x,y,z)&=x^2+y^2+7z^2,\\
h(x,y,z)&=x^2+y^2+14z^2,\\
k(x,y,z)&=x^2+2y^2+7z^2.
\end{aligned}
\]

1. The genus of determinant seven consists of \(f\) and \(g\), and together
   these two forms represent every 7-eligible positive integer.
2. The corresponding determinant-fourteen genus consists of \(h\) and
   \(k\), has the same eligible integers, and together these two forms
   represent every eligible positive integer.

This is the only genus-level input used in the determinant-seven argument.
Kaplansky states the first assertion and the eligibility criterion in
Section 1 of his paper and the second in Section 3.  The underlying theorem
that all locally represented integers are represented by some class in the
genus is Burton Jones, [*The regularity of a genus of positive ternary
quadratic forms*](https://doi.org/10.1090/S0002-9947-1931-1501578-8).

The next two subsections derive exactly the portion of Kaplansky's Theorems 1
and 3 needed here.  They are included so that “apply Kaplansky” does not hide
the transfer arguments.

### 2.2 Eligible integers in two residue classes modulo three

#### Lemma 2.1

The form \(k=x^2+2y^2+7z^2\) represents every 7-eligible positive integer
congruent to \(0\) or \(1\pmod3\).

**Proof.**
Let \(N\) be eligible and congruent to \(0\) or \(1\pmod3\).  By Classical
Result D7, either \(k\) represents \(N\), in which case we are done, or
\[
N=h(x,y,z)=x^2+y^2+14z^2
\]
for some integers \(x,y,z\).

Squares modulo three are \(0\) or \(1\).  We claim that at least one of
\(x^2,y^2\) equals \(z^2\) modulo three.  If not, then either

- \(z^2\equiv1\) and \(x^2\equiv y^2\equiv0\), or
- \(z^2\equiv0\) and \(x^2\equiv y^2\equiv1\).

In either case
\[
h(x,y,z)\equiv x^2+y^2+2z^2\equiv2\pmod3,
\]
contrary to the hypothesis on \(N\).

After interchanging \(x\) and \(y\) if necessary, and changing the sign of
\(x\) if necessary, we may therefore assume
\[
x\equiv-z\pmod3.
\]
Both \(x+7z\) and \(x-2z\) are divisible by three.  The identity
\[
x^2+y^2+14z^2
=y^2+2\left(\frac{x+7z}{3}\right)^2
  +7\left(\frac{x-2z}{3}\right)^2
\]
is verified by expansion:
\[
2(x+7z)^2+7(x-2z)^2
=9x^2+126z^2.
\]
Thus the displayed fractions are integers and give a representation of
\(N\) by \(k\). \(\square\)

This is Pall's one-page argument, with the congruence step and division by
three written out.

#### Lemma 2.2

For every integer \(A\),
\[
g\text{ represents }A
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
k\text{ represents }2A.
\]

**Proof.**
The identity
\[
2g(x,y,z)=2x^2+(2y+z)^2+7z^2
\]
shows that a representation of \(A\) by \(g\) yields
\[
k(2y+z,x,z)=2A.
\]

Conversely, suppose
\[
k(u,v,z)=u^2+2v^2+7z^2=2A.
\]
Reduction modulo two shows that \(u\) and \(z\) have the same parity.  Hence
\(y=(u-z)/2\) is an integer, \(u=2y+z\), and
\[
2g(v,y,z)=2v^2+u^2+7z^2=2A.
\]
Cancellation of the factor two in the integers gives \(g(v,y,z)=A\).
\(\square\)

#### Corollary 2.3

The form \(g\) represents every 7-eligible positive integer congruent to
\(0\) or \(2\pmod3\).

**Proof.**
Let \(A\) be such an integer.  Multiplication by two does not change the
7-adic valuation, and \(2\) is a quadratic residue modulo seven
(\(3^2\equiv2\pmod7\)).  Thus \(2A\) is also 7-eligible.  Moreover,
\[
A\equiv0,2\pmod3
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
2A\equiv0,1\pmod3.
\]
Lemma 2.1 says that \(k\) represents \(2A\), and Lemma 2.2 transfers that
representation to \(g\). \(\square\)

This is Kaplansky's Theorem 1.

### 2.3 Eligible integers in two residue classes modulo four

#### Lemma 2.4

The form \(g\) represents every 7-eligible positive integer congruent to
\(0\) or \(1\pmod4\).

**Proof.**
Let \(B\) be eligible and \(B\equiv0\) or \(1\pmod4\).  By Classical Result
D7, either \(g\) represents \(B\), or \(f\) represents \(B\).  It is enough
to prove that in the latter case a representation by \(f\) can be converted
to one by \(g\).

The conversion is based on
\[
f(x,w-2z,w)=g(x,2w-z,z). \tag{2.1}
\]
Expansion gives the common value
\[
x^2+8w^2-4wz+4z^2.
\]
The form \(g\) also has the integral automorphism
\[
g(x,-y-z,z)=g(x,y,z). \tag{2.2}
\]

We first record the reverse transfer, because it explains why the congruence
restriction is exactly modulo four.  Suppose \(B=g(u,v,w)\).

- If \(v\) and \(w\) have the same parity, put
  \(W=(v+w)/2\).  Then \(v=2W-w\), so (2.1) gives a representation by \(f\).
- If \(v\) is even and \(w\) is odd, (2.2) replaces \(v\) by
  \(-v-w\), which is odd; the preceding case then applies.
- The only remaining parity pattern is \(v\) odd and \(w\) even.  In that
  case
  \[
  g(u,v,w)\equiv u^2+2\pmod4,
  \]
  which is \(2\) or \(3\pmod4\), not \(0\) or \(1\).

Therefore every representation by \(g\) of a number \(0\) or \(1\pmod4\)
can be transferred to \(f\).

Now suppose \(B=f(u,v,w)\).  If \(v,w\) have the same parity, (2.1), read
from left to right, gives a representation by \(g\).  Since \(f\) is
symmetric in \(u,v\), the same is true if \(u,w\) have the same parity.
If neither pair has the same parity, exactly two cases remain:

- \(u,v\) odd and \(w\) even, giving \(f(u,v,w)\equiv2\pmod4\);
- \(u,v\) even and \(w\) odd, giving \(f(u,v,w)\equiv7\equiv3\pmod4\).

Both contradict \(B\equiv0\) or \(1\pmod4\).  Hence a representation by
\(f\) transfers to one by \(g\), completing the proof. \(\square\)

This is the part of Kaplansky's Theorem 3 needed here.

### 2.4 The conservative represented subset used below

For any integer \(a\), define \(\operatorname{Safe}_7(a)\) by
\[
\begin{cases}
a\not\equiv7,10\pmod {12},\\
a\not\equiv0,21,35,42\pmod {49}.
\end{cases}
\]

The second line is a deliberately conservative finite test:

- if \(7\nmid a\), then \(a\) is 7-eligible;
- if \(a\equiv7,14,28\pmod {49}\), its 7-adic valuation is one and its unit
  part is \(1,2,4\pmod7\), hence a quadratic residue;
- all other multiples of seven modulo 49 are rejected.

In particular, multiples of 49 are rejected even though many are eligible.
This loss of strength makes the later CRT check larger but keeps it entirely
modulo 588.

#### Proposition 2.5

Every positive integer \(a\) satisfying \(\operatorname{Safe}_7(a)\) is
represented by \(g\).

**Proof.**
The modulo-49 condition implies that \(a\) is 7-eligible by the preceding
case analysis.  If \(a\equiv0\) or \(2\pmod3\), Corollary 2.3 applies.  If
not, then \(a\equiv1\pmod3\).

The only residues modulo twelve that are simultaneously
\[
a\equiv1\pmod3
\quad\text{and}\quad
a\equiv2\text{ or }3\pmod4
\]
are \(10\) and \(7\), respectively.  These are excluded by
\(\operatorname{Safe}_7(a)\).  Hence in the remaining case
\(a\equiv0\) or \(1\pmod4\), and Lemma 2.4 applies. \(\square\)

This proposition is the complete determinant-seven ternary interface used
in Sections 3--6.  We never use the false assertion that \(g\) represents
every locally represented integer.

## 3. Exhaustive residual congruence covers

All conditions in \(\operatorname{Safe}_7\) are determined modulo
\[
\operatorname{lcm}(12,49)=588.
\]

### Lemma 3.1: the six generic residual covers

Let
\[
M=\{7,238,266,280,287,329\}.
\]
Suppose \(m\in M\), \(n\) is squarefree, and
\(\operatorname{Safe}_7(n)\) is false.  Then one of the choices of \(t\) in
the corresponding row below satisfies
\[
\operatorname{Safe}_7(n-mt^2).
\]

| \(m\) | sufficient values of \(t\) | largest \(mt^2\) |
| ---: | --- | ---: |
| \(7\) | \(1,2,3,4,6\) | \(252\) |
| \(266\) | \(1,2,3,4,5,6\) | \(9576\) |
| \(280\) | \(1,2,3,4,6,9\) | \(22680\) |
| \(329\) | \(1,2,3,4,6,9\) | \(26649\) |
| \(238\) | \(1,2,3,4,5,6\) | \(8568\) |
| \(287\) | \(1,2,3,4,6,9\) | \(23247\) |

**Proof.**
Because \(n\) is squarefree,
\[
n\not\equiv0\pmod4,\qquad n\not\equiv0\pmod {49}.
\]
It is therefore enough to inspect the 588 possible residues \(a\) and retain
those satisfying
\[
a\not\equiv0\pmod4,\quad
a\not\equiv0\pmod {49},\quad
\neg\operatorname{Safe}_7(a).
\]
There are 117 such source residues.

For each source residue, test the displayed values of \(t\) in order.  The
numbers first solved by successive choices are:

| \(m\) | numbers first solved by the successive \(t\)'s |
| ---: | --- |
| \(7\) | \(100,10,5,1,1\) |
| \(266\) | \(94,9,10,1,2,1\) |
| \(280\) | \(93,9,7,4,2,2\) |
| \(329\) | \(94,9,9,2,1,2\) |
| \(238\) | \(94,9,10,1,2,1\) |
| \(287\) | \(93,9,8,3,2,2\) |

Each row sums to 117, so every possible source residue is covered.  The
logical core of the exhaustive check is:

```text
for a in {0, ..., 587}:
  if a mod 4 = 0 or a mod 49 = 0:
    continue
  if Safe7(a):
    continue
  assert some displayed t has Safe7(a - m*t*t)
```

The executable checker also asserts the six count vectors above.
\(\square\)

The lemma is purely a congruence statement; \(n-mt^2\) may be negative.
Later we impose \(n>mt^2\) before applying Proposition 2.5.  This distinction
is why the last column is part of the statement.

### Lemma 3.2: the exceptional odd residual cover

Let \(n\) be odd and squarefree, and suppose
\(\neg\operatorname{Safe}_7(n)\).  Then one of
\[
t=1,2,3,4,5,6
\]
satisfies
\[
\operatorname{Safe}_7(n-315t^2).
\]

**Proof.**
The same modulo-588 enumeration now retains only odd source residues.  There
are 63.  The successive choices \(1,2,3,4,5,6\) first solve
\[
48,5,6,1,2,1
\]
residues, respectively.  These counts sum to 63. \(\square\)

## 4. The seven quaternary forms and their orthogonal vectors

For integers \(r,c\), define
\[
\begin{aligned}
Q_{r,c}(x,y,z,w)
  &=g(x,y,z)+2ryw+cw^2\\
  &=x^2+2y^2+2yz+4z^2+2ryw+cw^2.
\end{aligned}
\]
Its Gram matrix is
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&0&0\\
0&2&1&r\\
0&1&4&0\\
0&r&0&c
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The seven forms under consideration are
\[
(r,c)=(0,7),(1,6),(1,7),(2,8),(2,9),(3,10),(3,11).
\]

Write \(e_x,e_y,e_z,e_w\) for the standard basis.

### Lemma 4.1: integral orthogonal complements

If \(r\ne0\), the vector
\[
v_{r,c}=7e_w-4r e_y+r e_z
\]
is orthogonal to \(e_x,e_y,e_z\), and
\[
Q_{r,c}(v_{r,c})=49c-28r^2=7(7c-4r^2).
\]
If \(r=0\), the vector \(e_w\) is orthogonal to the ternary section and has
norm \(c\).

**Proof.**
Orthogonality to \(e_x\) is immediate.  The other two inner products are
\[
\langle v_{r,c},e_y\rangle=7r-8r+r=0
\]
and
\[
\langle v_{r,c},e_z\rangle=-4r+4r=0.
\]
For the norm, substitute \(x=0,y=-4r,z=r,w=7\):
\[
\begin{aligned}
Q_{r,c}(v_{r,c})
 &=2(16r^2)+2(-4r)(r)+4r^2+2r(-4r)(7)+49c\\
 &=32r^2-8r^2+4r^2-56r^2+49c\\
 &=49c-28r^2.
\end{aligned}
\]
The \(r=0\) assertion follows directly from the matrix. \(\square\)

Thus \(Q_{r,c}\) contains an integral orthogonal sublattice
\[
g\perp\langle m\rangle
\]
with the following \(m\):

| \((r,c)\) | \(m\) | \(m\bmod12\) | \(m\bmod49\) |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| \((0,7)\) | \(7\) | \(7\) | \(7\) |
| \((1,6)\) | \(266\) | \(2\) | \(21\) |
| \((1,7)\) | \(315\) | \(3\) | \(21\) |
| \((2,8)\) | \(280\) | \(4\) | \(35\) |
| \((2,9)\) | \(329\) | \(5\) | \(35\) |
| \((3,10)\) | \(238\) | \(10\) | \(42\) |
| \((3,11)\) | \(287\) | \(11\) | \(42\) |

The six forms other than \(Q_{1,7}\) use Lemma 3.1 directly.  The congruence
\(315\equiv3\pmod {12}\) is exactly the exceptional case isolated by
Bhargava's Table 2.

## 5. Universality of the six generic forms

### Theorem 5.1

The forms
\[
Q_{0,7},Q_{1,6},Q_{2,8},Q_{2,9},Q_{3,10},Q_{3,11}
\]
are universal.

**Proof.**
Fix one of the six forms and suppose it is not universal.  Let \(n\) be its
least positive unrepresented integer.  By Lemma 1.1, \(n\) is squarefree.

The quaternary form contains \(g\) as the section \(w=0\).  Therefore \(g\)
cannot represent \(n\); otherwise the same vector with \(w=0\) would
represent \(n\) by \(Q_{r,c}\).  Proposition 2.5, by contraposition, gives
\[
\neg\operatorname{Safe}_7(n).
\]

Let \(m\) be the norm in the corresponding row of the table in Section 4.
Lemma 3.1 supplies a displayed integer \(t\) such that
\[
\operatorname{Safe}_7(n-mt^2).
\]
Let \(B\) be the largest \(mt^2\) in that row of Lemma 3.1.

If \(n>B\), then \(n-mt^2>0\).  Proposition 2.5 says that \(g\) represents
this residual.  Lemma 4.1 supplies an integral vector of norm \(m\)
orthogonal to the copy of \(g\), and Lemma 1.2 lifts the residual
representation to a representation of \(n\) by \(Q_{r,c}\).  This
contradicts the choice of \(n\).

If \(n\le B\), the exhaustive finite check in Section 7 gives a direct
integral representation of \(n\) by \(Q_{r,c}\), again a contradiction.
Thus no least exception exists. \(\square\)

## 6. The exceptional form \(Q_{1,7}\)

The form is
\[
Q_{1,7}(x,y,z,w)
=x^2+2y^2+2yz+4z^2+2yw+7w^2.
\]
Its Gram matrix is
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&0&0\\
0&2&1&1\\
0&1&4&0\\
0&1&0&7
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Bhargava handles even and odd targets with two different ternary sections.
We do the same.

### 6.1 The alternative even ternary section

#### Lemma 6.1

The vectors
\[
a=e_y,\qquad b=2e_x,\qquad d=e_x+e_y-e_z-e_w
\]
span a sublattice \(K\) with Gram matrix
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
2&0&0\\
0&4&2\\
0&2&10
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The vector
\[
s=e_y-4e_z+2e_w
\]
is orthogonal to \(K\) and has norm \(90\).

**Proof.**
Using the displayed Gram matrix of \(Q_{1,7}\),
\[
Q(a)=2,\qquad Q(b)=4,\qquad Q(d)=10,
\]
and
\[
\langle a,b\rangle=0,\qquad
\langle a,d\rangle=0,\qquad
\langle b,d\rangle=2.
\]
These six values give the claimed Gram matrix.

For the orthogonal vector,
\[
\langle s,a\rangle=2-4+2=0,
\qquad
\langle s,b\rangle=0,
\]
and direct substitution also gives \(\langle s,d\rangle=0\).  Finally,
\[
Q(s)=2+64+28-8+4=90.
\]
All vectors are integral, so \(K\perp\langle90\rangle\) is an integral
sublattice of \(Q_{1,7}\). \(\square\)

The quadratic polynomial of \(K\) is
\[
K(X,Y,Z)=2X^2+4Y^2+4YZ+10Z^2.
\]
In particular, \(K\) represents only even integers.  Any claim that it
represents admissible odd integers would be false.

### 6.2 Why the even section is controlled by three squares

We now use the second and final classical input.

### Legendre's three-square theorem

A positive integer \(N\) is a sum of three integral squares if and only if
\[
N\ne4^a(8b+7)
\]
for all integers \(a,b\ge0\).

A concise modern proof is N. C. Ankeny, [*Sums of three
squares*](https://doi.org/10.1090/S0002-9939-1957-0085275-8).  The current
formal library proves the obstruction direction and the integral descent;
the rational-existence part of the converse remains an explicit interface.

Put
\[
H(x,y,z)=x^2+2y^2+2yz+5z^2.
\]

#### Lemma 6.2

An integer \(N\) is represented by \(H\) if and only if it is a sum of three
integer squares.

**Proof.**
One direction is the identity
\[
H(x,y,z)=x^2+(y+2z)^2+(y-z)^2. \tag{6.1}
\]

Conversely, suppose
\[
N=a^2+b^2+c^2.
\]
Modulo three, every square is \(0\) or \(1\).  Among the three squares
\(a^2,b^2,c^2\), two are therefore equal modulo three.  Permute the
coordinates so that those two roots are \(p,q\), and call the remaining root
\(x\).

The congruence \(p^2\equiv q^2\pmod3\) implies
\[
(p-q)(p+q)\equiv0\pmod3.
\]
Thus, after replacing \(p\) by \(-p\) if necessary, we may assume
\(p\equiv q\pmod3\).  Define
\[
z=\frac{p-q}{3},
\qquad
y=q+z.
\]
These are integers, and
\[
y+2z=q+3z=p,\qquad y-z=q.
\]
Substitution in (6.1) gives
\[
H(x,y,z)=x^2+p^2+q^2=N.
\]
\(\square\)

Since \(K=2H\), this immediately gives the exact statement needed for even
targets.

#### Corollary 6.3

Let \(N>0\) be even.  Then \(K\) represents \(N\) if and only if
\[
N\ne2^{\,2a+1}(8b+7)
\]
for all \(a,b\ge0\).

**Proof.**
Write \(N=2M\).  The form \(K\) represents \(N\) exactly when \(H\)
represents \(M\), which by Lemma 6.2 and Legendre's theorem is exactly when
\(M\ne4^a(8b+7)\).  Multiplying by two gives the displayed forbidden
shape. \(\square\)

This is the meaning of Bhargava's notation \(2^d u_7\) in the daggered row
of Table 2: \(d\) denotes an odd exponent and \(u_7\) an integer congruent
to \(7\pmod8\).  The restriction to even targets is essential and is stated
explicitly in Bhargava's dagger footnote.

### 6.3 Even least exceptions

#### Lemma 6.4

If \(n\) is the least positive integer not represented by \(Q_{1,7}\) and
\(n\) is even, then \(n\le90\).

**Proof.**
By Lemma 1.1, \(n\) is squarefree.  If \(K\) represents \(n\), then the
inclusion \(K\subset Q_{1,7}\) contradicts the choice of \(n\).  Otherwise
Corollary 6.3 says
\[
n=2^{\,2a+1}(8b+7).
\]
Squarefreeness forces \(2a+1=1\), so
\[
n\equiv14\pmod {16}.
\]

Suppose \(n>90\).  Then \(n-90>0\) and
\[
n-90\equiv14-10\equiv4\pmod {16}.
\]
Hence \(n-90\) has 2-adic valuation exactly two.  In particular, it is not
of the forbidden shape in Corollary 6.3, whose 2-adic valuation is odd.
Therefore \(K\) represents \(n-90\).  Lemma 6.1 and the orthogonal residual
principle lift this to a representation of \(n\) by \(Q_{1,7}\), a
contradiction. \(\square\)

### 6.4 Odd least exceptions

The original ternary section \(g\) is orthogonal to the norm-\(315\) vector
\[
v_{1,7}=7e_w-4e_y+e_z
\]
from Lemma 4.1.

#### Theorem 6.5

The form \(Q_{1,7}\) is universal.

**Proof.**
Suppose not, and let \(n\) be its least positive unrepresented integer.
Then \(n\) is squarefree.

If \(n\) is even and \(n>90\), Lemma 6.4 gives a contradiction.

Suppose \(n\) is odd.  The section \(w=0\) is \(g\), so \(g\) cannot
represent \(n\).  Proposition 2.5 implies
\[
\neg\operatorname{Safe}_7(n).
\]
Lemma 3.2 supplies \(t\in\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\) such that
\[
\operatorname{Safe}_7(n-315t^2).
\]
If
\[
n>315\cdot6^2=11340,
\]
this residual is positive.  Proposition 2.5 represents it by \(g\), and the
orthogonal norm-\(315\) vector lifts the representation to \(n\), a
contradiction.

It remains to consider:

- even \(n\le90\); and
- odd \(n\le11340\).

The finite verification in Section 7 checks every target
\(1\le n\le11340\), so both sets are represented.  Therefore no least
exception exists. \(\square\)

## 7. Exhaustive finite verification

This section explains why the finite search checks every possible
representation in the claimed range rather than merely searching a
heuristic box.

Fix \(Q_{r,c}\), and put
\[
h(y,z,w)=2y^2+2yz+4z^2+2ryw+cw^2.
\]
Then
\[
Q_{r,c}(x,y,z,w)=x^2+h(y,z,w).
\]
Let
\[
D=7c-4r^2.
\]
For all seven forms, \(D>0\).  If
\[
u=2y+z+rw,
\]
then direct expansion gives the exact identity
\[
2h
=u^2+\frac{(7z-rw)^2}{7}+\frac{2D}{7}w^2. \tag{7.1}
\]
Although fractions occur in (7.1), the equality is an equality of rational
numbers obtained from an integral expression; it is used only for
inequalities.

Every term on the right is nonnegative.  Thus a representation of a target
at most \(B\) must have \(0\le h\le B\), and (7.1) implies
\[
\begin{aligned}
D w^2&\le7B,\\
(7z-rw)^2&\le14B,\\
(2y+z+rw)^2&\le2B.
\end{aligned} \tag{7.2}
\]
These are exhaustive bounds on \(w,z,y\).

The checker uses integer intervals rounded outward from (7.2), discards
triples whose exact value of \(h\) is outside \([0,B]\), records one triple
for every attained \(h\), and then adds every square \(x^2\le B-h\).  In
pseudocode:

```text
attainedH[0 ... B] := false
represented[0 ... B] := false

enumerate every integer (w,z,y) satisfying the three outward-rounded
bounds in (7.2):
  h := 2*y*y + 2*y*z + 4*z*z + 2*r*y*w + c*w*w
  if 0 <= h <= B:
    attainedH[h] := true

for h in {0, ..., B} with attainedH[h]:
  for x in {0, ..., floor(sqrt(B-h))}:
    represented[h + x*x] := true

assert represented[n] for every n in {1, ..., B}
```

No sign of \(x\) is needed because only \(x^2\) occurs.  If \(h>B\), then
\(h+x^2>B\) for every integer \(x\), so discarding that triple cannot lose a
representation in the target interval.

The verified bounds are:

| form | bound \(B\) | triples with \(0\le h\le B\) | missing targets |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| \(Q_{0,7}\) | \(252\) | \(2387\) | none |
| \(Q_{1,6}\) | \(9576\) | \(636475\) | none |
| \(Q_{2,8}\) | \(22680\) | \(2262013\) | none |
| \(Q_{2,9}\) | \(26649\) | \(2657751\) | none |
| \(Q_{3,10}\) | \(8568\) | \(569451\) | none |
| \(Q_{3,11}\) | \(23247\) | \(2318499\) | none |
| \(Q_{1,7}\) | \(11340\) | \(753905\) | none |

The executable checker additionally:

1. verifies every orthogonality and norm calculation in Sections 4 and 6;
2. reconstructs the Gram matrix of the alternative section \(K\);
3. reruns all seven CRT covers and checks their count vectors;
4. stores and re-evaluates an explicit four-coordinate witness for every
   target in every finite interval; and
5. fails immediately if any expected count or representation changes.

The checker is an audit artifact, not a kernel certificate.  During
formalization, the finite witness generator should emit explicit witnesses
in small modules and a drift check, following the existing generated-cover
pattern in the library.

## 9. Reviewer checklist

The following are the places where a compressed proof is most likely to hide
an error.

1. **Gram convention.**  An off-diagonal Gram entry \(r\) contributes
   \(2ryw\) to the quadratic polynomial.
2. **Minimality.**  Squarefreeness applies to a least exception, not to an
   arbitrary target.
3. **Nonregular parent.**  Proposition 2.5 is one-way.  Its converse is
   neither stated nor used.
4. **Modulo-49 conservatism.**  \(\operatorname{Safe}_7\) rejects every
   multiple of 49; this enlarges finite bounds but cannot create an invalid
   representation claim.
5. **Positivity of residuals.**  The CRT lemmas alone do not make residuals
   positive.  Positivity is supplied separately by the explicit bounds.
6. **Exceptional section parity.**  \(K\) represents only even integers.
   Corollary 6.3 is correspondingly restricted to even targets.
7. **Three-square equivalence.**  The reverse map in Lemma 6.2 requires a
   sign choice and divisibility by three; both are proved.
8. **Finite-search completeness.**  The search bounds follow from the
   positive decomposition (7.1), and every returned witness is re-evaluated.
9. **Global scope.**  Completing these seven rank-four covers is not the
   same as formalizing every classical ternary converse in Section 8.
